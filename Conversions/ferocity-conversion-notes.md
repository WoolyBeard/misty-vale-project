# Ferocity

Ferocity is the single most important value on a monster. It determines how many times the creature acts each round, affecting not only its damage output but also how long it survives. Every additional action forces the heroes to spend more of their own actions dodging or parrying instead of attacking, increasing the monster's effective durability.

> Don't choose Ferocity based on how "powerful" a creature is choose it based on how the encounter should feel.

---

## A Good Rule of Thumb

- **Ferocity 1** — Creatures that appear in groups, such as wolves, skeletons, goblins, or other rank-and-file enemies. Also suitable for most NPCs and humanoids.
- **Ferocity 2** — Monsters that are expected to fight the party alone or with only minor support. This is a good default for solos.
- **Ferocity = Number of Players − 1** — A challenging but fair solo encounter. Many official monsters follow this guideline.
- **Ferocity 4+** — Reserve for legendary creatures and climactic bosses. Every point of Ferocity dramatically increases a monster's presence and difficulty.

---

## Design Before You Convert

Before assigning Ferocity, ask what role the monster fills.

- Is it a glass cannon that overwhelms with speed?
- A brute that slowly grinds the party down?
- An ambush predator that strikes and retreats?
- A controller that dominates the battlefield?
- A relentless hunter that never lets the heroes recover?

Ferocity should reinforce that identity. A nimble predator may have higher Ferocity than a giant lumbering beast, even if the latter has far more Hit Points.

---

## Remember: Ferocity Is More Than Damage

A monster with **Ferocity 3** does not simply deal three times as much damage as one with **Ferocity 1**.

Each additional action also pressures the party's defenses. Players often spend actions dodging or parrying instead of attacking, meaning a high-Ferocity monster naturally survives longer because the heroes have fewer opportunities to strike back.

Think of Ferocity as a measure of a monster's battlefield presence, not just its offensive power. It represents how relentlessly the creature keeps the initiative, forces reactions, and controls the pace of combat.

> When in doubt, err on the side of **lower Ferocity and higher Hit Points** for slow, resilient monsters, and **higher Ferocity with fewer Hit Points** for fast, dangerous ones. This creates encounters that feel distinct rather than simply tougher.

---

# Converting Ferocity

Unlike Hit Points or Armor, **Ferocity cannot be calculated from an OSE stat block**. It is a judgement call that determines how often the monster acts each round and, therefore, how the encounter feels.

Before assigning Ferocity, ask yourself:

- Will this creature usually be encountered alone or in groups?
- Is it fast, aggressive, or relentless, or is it slow and methodical?
- Is it meant to overwhelm the party with repeated attacks, or withstand punishment through toughness alone?
- What role does it fill: predator, brute, ambusher, skirmisher, controller, or boss?

Choose Ferocity to match that role rather than the monster's Hit Dice.

---

## General Guidelines

| Ferocity | Typical Creatures |
|----------:|-------------------|
| **1** | Humanoids, animals, undead, and monsters commonly encountered in groups. Slow brutes and creatures that rely on a single powerful attack each round. |
| **2** | Elite monsters, dangerous predators, and creatures expected to fight the party alone or with only minor allies. A good default for most solo monsters. |
| **3** | Dragons, hydras, demons, giants, and other monsters intended to dominate an encounter. Use when a creature's speed, multiple limbs, or supernatural abilities justify acting repeatedly. |
| **4+** | Legendary beings and final bosses. Reserve for monsters that should define the entire battle. |

---

## Solo Monsters

If a monster is expected to fight the party by itself, a useful rule of thumb is:

> **Ferocity = Number of Player Characters − 1**

This produces a difficult but generally fair encounter and closely matches many official Dragonbane monsters.

---

## Use Description, Not Statistics

An OSE monster's Hit Dice, Armor Class, and damage are already converted elsewhere. **Do not increase Ferocity simply because a monster has many Hit Dice.**

For example:

- An ogre may have high HP but only **Ferocity 1** because it is slow and relies on heavy swings.
- A troll is relentless and difficult to pin down, making **Ferocity 2** appropriate.
- A giant spider may have modest HP but **Ferocity 2 or 3**, darting in, striking, and repositioning before the party can react.
- A dragon earns high Ferocity because it flies, breathes fire, claws, bites, and controls the battlefield—not merely because it is powerful.

---

## When in Doubt

If you're unsure, start with **Ferocity 1**.

Increase it only if you want the monster to:

- Keep constant pressure on the party.
- Force frequent dodges and parries.
- Act multiple times before the heroes can respond.
- Feel fast, relentless, or overwhelming.

> Remember that every point of Ferocity greatly increases a monster's effective power. Additional actions mean more attacks, more movement, and more opportunities to force defensive reactions, making the monster significantly more dangerous than its Hit Points alone would suggest.
